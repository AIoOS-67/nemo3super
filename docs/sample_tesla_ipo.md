## Key Facts

- **IPO Date:** June 29, 2010
- **Exchange:** NASDAQ (ticker: **TSLA**)
- **IPO Price:** $17.00 per share
- **Shares Offered:** 13.3 million
- **Capital Raised:** ~$226 million
- **Valuation at IPO:** ~$1.7 billion
- **Lead Underwriters:** Goldman Sachs, Morgan Stanley, J.P. Morgan, Deutsche Bank
- **First Day Close:** $23.89 (+40.5%)

## Context at the Time

Tesla was the first U.S. automaker to go public since Ford in 1956. The company was
unprofitable, had delivered only ~1,000 Roadsters, and depended heavily on a $465M
DOE loan. Most analysts were skeptical — Tesla's S-1 admitted it had **never been
profitable** and might never be.

Elon Musk personally bought $15M worth of shares at the IPO price.

## What the Bears Said in 2010

- "Niche luxury EV maker, no path to mass market."
- "Burns cash too fast — will need constant dilutive raises."
- "Detroit and Toyota will crush them when they decide to enter EVs."
- "Valuation already prices in perfection."

## What Actually Happened (2010 → 2024)

| Metric | 2010 IPO | 2024 |
|--------|----------|------|
| Share price (split-adjusted) | $1.13 | ~$240 |
| Annual deliveries | ~1,000 | ~1.8 million |
| Revenue | $117M | ~$97B |
| Market cap | $1.7B | ~$760B |

A $10,000 IPO investment held to 2024 would be worth roughly **$2.1 million** —
a return of ~21,000%, dwarfing the S&P 500's ~5x over the same period.

## Lessons / Takeaways

1. **Founder skin in the game matters.** Musk buying $15M at IPO signaled conviction.
2. **The market repeatedly mispriced the option value** of vertical integration
   (batteries, software, charging network, manufacturing).
3. **Bear theses can be directionally right and still wrong on magnitude** — yes,
   Tesla diluted shareholders many times, but the per-share value still compounded.
4. **A "first" can become a moat.** Being the first credible EV pure-play gave Tesla
   talent gravity, brand, and supply-chain priority that legacy OEMs spent a decade
   trying to replicate.
